The Laser Interferometer Gravitational-wave Observatory (LIGO for short), is a scientific project to use gravitational waves to learn about our Universe.


LIGO have two detectors in the USA, which are like giant versions of the model interferometer you see here. There are plans for more detectors across the world.

The University of Birmingham is a member of the LIGO Collaboration. Researchers from Birmingham designed and built parts for the detectors. They also analyse the gravitational-wave signals to learn about black holes and gravity itself.


The picture shows parts of the LIGO gravitational wave detector. Hundreds of these were made in Birmingham and are used in the detector today.

Gravity is the force which causes apples to fall from trees, keeps the Moon orbiting around the Earth and the Earth orbiting the Sun.


Gravity is the most important force when studying space, black holes and gravitational waves (of course)!


What did Einstein think about gravity? Click next to find out.

Albert Einstein came up with his theory of general relativity in 1915, over 100 years ago.


He explained gravity as the curving of space. Space is curved by massive things, and objects move following this curvature.


Click next to find out about black holes and gravitational waves.

Black holes were predicted by general relativity. They are regions of extremely strong gravity. Close to a black hole, gravity is so strong that nothing can escape, not even light!


Black holes can be born when massive stars, much bigger than our Sun, collapse.


Click next to find out about gravitational waves.

Another prediction of general relativity are gravitational waves.


When massive objects accelerate, the curvature of space changes. It takes time for these changes to move out, like ripples across a pond. These ripples in space are gravitational waves.

14 September 2015: The two LIGO detectors make the first direct observation of gravitational waves.


After 40 years of work on gravitational-wave detectors, we are able to confirm a prediction Einstein made 100 years ago and discover more about black holes.


Where did these gravitational waves come from? Click next to find out.

The gravitational waves came from a binary black hole merger. This means that two black holes orbited each other, spiralled together and formed a single, larger black hole.


How big were the black holes? Click next to find out and see what we can learn from gravitational waves.


Gravitational waves are a brand new way to do astronomy. They've told us that not only do black holes really exist, but they exist in pairs which collide! These collisions emit gravitational waves exactly as Einstein predicted.


Gravitational waves tell us things we never knew before, like where black holes are in the sky, and how heavy they are.

Colliding black holes are not the only objects which create gravitational waves. We can learn more about our Universe by searching for gravitational waves created by different objects as well.

In the future, there are plans for more gravitational-wave detectors around the world and even in space!
