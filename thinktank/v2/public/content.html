Gravitational waves stretch and squash space.


However, this effect is incredibly tiny and so difficult to measure. We are now able to build laser interferometers sensitive enough to spot these effects.

The Laser Interferometer Gravitational-wave Observatory (LIGO for short), is a scientific project to use gravitational waves to learn about our Universe.


LIGO have two detectors in the USA, which are like giant versions of the model interferometer you see here. There are plans for more detectors across the world.

:

Virgo is a gravitational wave detector in Italy. Like LIGO it is a giant laser interferometer.


LIGO and Virgo work together to detect gravitational waves and learn about where they come from. Having more detectors helps us get more detailed information.

:

The University of Birmingham is a member of the LIGO Collaboration. Researchers from Birmingham designed and built parts for the detectors. They also analyse the gravitational-wave signals to learn about black holes and gravity itself.


The picture shows parts of the LIGO gravitational wave detector. Hundreds of these were made in Birmingham and are used in the detector today.

Gravity is the force which causes apples to fall from trees, keeps the Moon orbiting around the Earth and the Earth orbiting the Sun.


Gravity is the most important force when studying space, black holes and gravitational waves (of course)!


What did Einstein think about gravity? Click next to find out.

:

Albert Einstein came up with his theory of general relativity in 1915, over 100 years ago.


He explained gravity as the curving of space. Space is curved by massive things, and objects move following this curvature.


Click next to find out about massive stars.

:

The more material in an object, the strong its gravity.


Massive stars can be many times as big as our Sun. Their gravity is very strong. While they have fuel to burn, they can stay fluffy, but once it is used up they collapse under their own gravity.


Click next to find out about neutron stars

:

Neutron stars are the remains of collapsed massive stars. The strong gravity has squashed the star down to a ball of extremely dense material, similar to what is found in the nucleus of atoms..


Neutron stars about about twice the mass of our Sun, but only about 20 kilometres in diameter!


Click next to find out about black holes.

:

Black holes were predicted by general relativity. They are regions of extremely strong gravity. Close to a black hole, gravity is so strong that nothing can escape, not even light!


Black holes can be born when massive stars, much bigger than our Sun, collapse.


Click next to find out about gravitational waves.

:

Another prediction of general relativity are gravitational waves.


When massive objects accelerate, the curvature of space changes. It takes time for these changes to move out, like ripples across a pond. These ripples in space are gravitational waves.

:

14 September 2015: The two LIGO detectors make the first direct observation of gravitational waves.


After 40 years of work on gravitational-wave detectors, we are able to confirm a prediction Einstein made 100 years ago and discover more about black holes.


Where did these gravitational waves come from? Click next to find out.

:

The gravitational waves came from a binary black hole merger. This means that two black holes orbited each other, spiralled together and formed a single, larger black hole.


How big were the black holes? Click next to find out and see what we can learn from gravitational waves.

:


:

Gravitational waves are a brand new way to do astronomy. They've told us that not only do black holes really exist, but they exist in pairs which collide! These collisions emit gravitational waves exactly as Einstein predicted.


Gravitational waves tell us things we never knew before, like where black holes are in the sky, and how heavy they are.

:

As well as being able to detect gravitational waves from binary black hole inspirals, we can detect them from binary neutron stars, and a neutron star black hole collisions.


As of the end of the second observing run, we've seen binary black holes, and binary neutron stars collide and produce gravitational waves.


:

We continue to learn about the universe with gravitational waves, upgrading the detectors to improve the range and opperating time.


Colliding black holes and neutron stars are not the only objects which create gravitational waves. Who knows what we will discover next?

In the future, there are plans for more gravitational-wave detectors around the world and even in space!

The graphic to the right shows the timeline of events for the first detection. First the inspiral of the two black holes, this can take thousands, even millions of years. We detect the last 0.4 seconds of the inspiral. This is quickly followed by the merger, and finally the ringdown.

Aurore Simonnet, E/PO Sonoma State University

:

Since September 2015 we have now observed many more binary black hole mergers. This family of black holes comes in a range of different masses, from around 7 times the mass of our Sun to over 30 times.


Gravitational waves are a brand new way to do astronomy. We would not have found these binary black holes without them.

:

17 August 2017: The two LIGO detectors and the Virgo detector make their first observation of gravitational waves from a binary neutron star merger.


The signal is similar to that for a binary black hole merger, but because neutron stars are less massive, it lasts much longer.

NASA/Swift/Dana Berry

:

Binary black holes can only be observed with gravitational waves. However, when two neutron stars merge there is a huge explosion, which can be seen with telescopes.


Thanks to LIGO and Virgo, astronomers around the world saw light coming from the merger. We now know that gamma ray bursts, some of the most violent explosions in the Universe come from merging neutron stars, and that heavy elements like gold and platinum are made in these collisions.

The Dark Energy Camera GW-EM Collaboration and the DES Collaboration / PI: Berger; J. Najita, M. Newhouse & NOAO/AURA/NSF.

:

We will continue to learn about our Universe with gravitational waves. Colliding black holes and neutron stars are not the only objects which create gravitational waves. Who knows what we will discover next. In the future, there are plans for more gravitational wave detectors around the world and even in space!
